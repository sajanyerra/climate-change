This project was completed as part of module MI6201 – Data and Analytical Decision Making at the University of Limerick. Project Overview

What is This Project About?

This project explores the usage of data to construct an argument that climate change is not as severe as commonly portrayed. The aim was to demonstrate how data visualisation and selective framing can be used to create biased narratives — a critical skill for any data analyst to understand and identify.

Research Scope

Topics Covered

The analysis spans six key research areas, each using real datasets to construct a carefully framed narrative — illustrating the power and danger of selective data presentation.

🌡️

Temperature Deviation (1850–2022)

Analysing global temperature variability relative to the 20th-century mean. Framed to show that the maximum deviation of 1.03°C is within Paris Agreement targets and therefore not alarming.

🏭

Greenhouse Gas Emissions Over Time

CO₂ rose 20× from 1900 to 2021, yet temperature increases appear disproportionately small — used to argue a weak causal link between emissions and warming.

🇰🇷

South Korea as a Case Study

South Korea's GHG trends mirror global patterns, yet temperature deviations remain inconsistent — presented as further evidence against a strong CO₂-temperature relationship.

🌧️

Irish Precipitation Patterns

Rainfall data from Irish meteorological stations (1960–2020) shows no significant deviation, used to suggest GHG increases haven't impacted local climate patterns.

🌋

Natural Events: Volcanoes & ENSO

Volcanic eruptions and El Niño / La Niña cycles are highlighted to argue that natural forces are dominant drivers of climate change, not human activity.

🌿

Positive Impacts of Elevated CO₂

Elevated CO₂ boosts plant photosynthesis by ~40%, improves crop yields, and reduces cold-related deaths — framed to suggest climate change has net positive effects.

Summary

Our Conclusion


The climate is a dynamic, intricate system shaped by various natural forces. Over geological timeframes, Earth has experienced multiple climate variations caused by orbital movements, solar radiation fluctuations, and volcanic activity — factors that predate industrialisation by millions of years.

Although human activities such as deforestation and combustion of fossil fuels contribute to climate change, this project demonstrates how data can be framed to make their effects appear less significant when placed against a backdrop of natural climate variability.

The project also highlights often-overlooked positive feedback loops from elevated CO₂ — including enhanced crop productivity, reduced cold-related mortality, and short-term benefits to coral calcification — to show how cherry-picking data can create a compelling but incomplete picture.

Ultimately, this is an exercise in data literacy and critical thinking. Understanding how narratives are built from data is essential for any analyst — both to avoid creating misleading visualisations and to recognise them when encountered.
